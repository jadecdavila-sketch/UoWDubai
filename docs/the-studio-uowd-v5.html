I.
The Thesis
Bearing 252°

Pedagogy first.
Personalization is earned.

Every voice in the room — each a specialist in one craft of teaching — serves one sentence, and when a voice's best practice collides with it, the sentence wins.

◆ Draft thesis · for your reaction
A room of AI specialists — each expert in one craft of teaching — works your material into a single, finished course design. You approve it. It locks. That's the master. From that one master, every student gets their own edition: the same lesson underneath, their own way in. The master is the contract between you and them.
You guarantee what's taught. The system makes it personal. Generate a whole course on the fly and it falls apart — students need a shared spine to hold onto. So the personal is earned, not assumed: the teaching is built and approved first, and the edition is what arrives after. Never instead.

The long vision is yours: courses that meet each student where they are, in their language, through their world. The discipline is ours to encode: that vision only holds if the teaching underneath it is structurally sound. Trust, but verify, built into the architecture rather than appended to it.

III.
The Proof
Bearing 324°

The deck goes in.
The master comes out.

The room's drafters propose. Its reviewers push back, with named gaps. The loop runs until the room agrees — or until the disagreement is worth bringing to you.

1
The deck arrives
Your slides become the data room. Everything downstream is accountable to them.
2
The drafting seats
The Subject Expert shapes the teaching from the subject your course conveys; the Scriptwriter gives it a human voice. Two of the room's seats, chunk by chunk.
3
The room pushes back
Each evaluating voice critiques through its own lens, with named gaps. A failed check sends the chunk back to step 2.
4
The Design Chair weaves
Agreements, collisions, and open calls are synthesized into the master and its reasoning trace.
5
The house reviews
Before it reaches you, UOWD's own instructional designers vet the master against the institution's standards — reading the room's trace, not re-deriving it. They sign off, or send it back. At launch, every master. Once the room earns it, a sampled few.
6
The faculty reviews and decides
The faculty member's review, feedback, and final say. Approve, send it back with notes, redirect, or turn a voice up — the room's reasoning and the designers' sign-off open beneath the decision.

The trace is not an afterthought. Every script ships with its making-of: where the voices agreed, where they pulled apart, and which calls were left to you.

◆ The room works under your designers, not around them. Every master passes a human instructional designer before it passes to faculty — and your designers hold the dial that decides how much the system still needs watching. They are not replaced by the room. They are promoted above it.

IV.
The Master
Bearing 000° · True North

What is locked,
and what is free.

Each chunk carries its invariants: the concepts that must land, the misconceptions that must not, and the checkpoint that proves it. Everything else is edition.

Chunk 3 · Escalation of Commitment
An example chunk, from a Principles of Management master
Must land
Commitment escalates because of identity and self-justification, not arithmetic. The money is gone either way.
Must avoid
The misconception that sunk cost is only about money. Time, reputation, and public position sink too.
Checkpoint
The learner names one commitment in their own work or studies that deserves the test.
◆ Yours
Which example carries it, how hard the question lands, and whether this chunk is the place to be bold.

This is the answer to the hardest question in the room: how can a thousand students each get their own lecture without the course dissolving into a thousand courses? Because the skeleton is shared. Like a score that survives every performance, the invariants don't move — only the playing does. A thousand students. One course. Nothing essential drifts.

● Held in every edition: escalation is identity, not arithmetic · sunk cost is never only money · the learner names their own escalation trap. An edition that entertains but fails these checks fails, whatever costume it wears.
◆ Notice the seam line in the costumed editions. Every analogy is required to declare where it stops being the concept. Naming the seam is itself teaching, and it is how an edition stays medicine rather than entertainment.
The presenter, and the aside

The edition has a face. The Presenter is the talking-head avatar that delivers each chunk and, when the student raises a hand, stops. A question opens an aside: a bounded conversation inside the chunk, played by the same presenter under the same rules. Its answers trace to the master and the deck or wear the enrichment tag, and the invariants ride along, compiled into the presenter's instructions, because the full studio cannot convene on every spoken turn. The Presenter is not a seat at the table. It is how the master is delivered.

When a question runs past the edge of the master, the presenter says so plainly, and the question is routed to a ledger on the desk. That ledger may be the most valuable artifact in the system: the question every third student asks is the master's best critic, and in Phase III it walks back upstream into the proof room.

◆ Whether the presenter wears one persona per course, the faculty member's own licensed likeness, or a face the student chooses from an approved set is a governance call before it is a technical one. It is waiting for you in the open questions.

The Programme
Bearing 108°

The room gets smarter
by phase.

The product moves in your sequence: deck to script to video first, standalone first. What grows phase over phase is the data room the studio reasons from.

PHASE I · NOW
The deck is the data room
One faculty deck, one non-quantitative course, standalone. Chunks, scripts, faculty review, narrated video. Every claim traced or tagged. The harness does not need to be good yet. It needs to be honest.
The room holds: your slides
PHASE II
The course joins the room
LTI 1.3 into Moodle brings the course shell: syllabus, prior modules, assessment structure. Live regional job-market context connects concepts to practice. Arabic arrives as an edition dimension, in step with the national agenda.
The room holds: the course, the region
PHASE III
The studio turns upstream
The studio critiques the incoming deck itself against learning design principles before production begins, and learning signals from real cohorts feed the next proof. Your standards agenda, encoded and running.
The room holds: how students actually learned
◆ The human-ID dial moves with the phases. Every master reviewed at launch; sampled as the room proves out; and by Phase III, your designers set the standards upstream rather than checking course by course. The room earns autonomy. The institution sets the pace.
◆ One assumption to confirm: UOWD's Moodle appears to run on the parent university's platform, which would route LTI approval through central IT in Australia. If so, standalone-first is not a compromise. It is the only Phase I that does not wait on another continent.
VIII.
Where the Engine Stops
Bearing 144°

Left to the faculty's
judgment.

The room is built to know what it does not own. These calls are surfaced, named, and handed over. Deliberately.

◆ Calls the studio deliberately leaves to the faculty member
“
Is it true? Disciplinary accuracy is your lane, named as yours in the interface. The room checks structure, alignment, and provenance. It does not pretend to outrank you on the discipline itself.
“
Is it taught the way you would teach it? Taste, emphasis, and the choice of example are teaching decisions. The room proposes; the desk is yours.
“
How bold should this chunk be? The Provocateur argues for the uncomfortable question. Whether your room is ready for it is a judgment no model should make.
“
What gets cut? A master is also made of what it leaves out. The omissions are yours to own, with the room's reasoning open in front of you.
IX.
Taking Root
Bearing 180°

Built is not the same
as adopted.

A course line no one teaches through is just infrastructure. Adoption is earned the way trust is — in a sequence, by proof, at the institution's pace.

PHASE 0 · FOUNDATION
Name the coalition
Sponsor secured, your instructional designers brought in as partners, and two or three willing faculty teaching the courses where the time returned is obvious. Agree what success looks like before a single course is built.
House Review: setting the bar
PHASE 1 · LIGHTHOUSE
Prove it once, visibly
A handful of volunteers, high-enrolment courses, heavy support, every master reviewed. The goal is not volume. It is undeniable evidence — and a respected name willing to say out loud that it worked.
House Review: every master
PHASE 2 · EXPAND
Let proof spread
The early majority, pulled by their colleagues' results rather than pushed by mandate. Self-serve onboarding, Moodle integration, and real recognition for taking part.
House Review: sampled
PHASE 3 · EMBED
Make it the path
For the right course types, the studio becomes the default route inside programme development, and your designers set standards upstream. Adoption is no longer a project. It is how courses are made.
House Review: standards upstream
◆ The non-negotiable underneath all of it: the faculty member authors and approves, and nothing reaches a student unvetted. The day adoption feels like replacement, it stops. Ownership is not a courtesy here — it is the strategy.
◆ Your instructional designers are the engine, not a casualty. The House Review makes them the quality authority over the whole line, so they become its strongest advocates inside the building. Win them first.
X.
For Your Reaction
Bearing 216°

What we are not sure about,
on purpose.

A draft that cannot be argued with is not a draft. These are the questions we want this document to start.

i.
Is the craft seat's first tenant right?
We propose a Management mind, in your own discipline, where your eye is sharpest. Which course, and whose deck, should sit first?
ii.
Does inclusion hold a seat at the table?
Accessibility and the multilingual learner: a voice of their own, or a dimension of every edition? We lean toward a seat. Held for your reaction.
iii.
Where does the room sit first?
We have it drafting and critiquing at the script stage, moving upstream to the deck in Phase III. Is that the right order of arrival?
iv.
Where is Moodle administered?
Dubai or Australia. The answer sets the LTI approval path and the Phase II timeline.
v.
What must the pilot prove?
Hours returned to faculty, completion, satisfaction, or something you watch that we have not named.
vi.
Does assessment eventually join the studio?
Your public argument is that assessment must be reinvented toward demonstrated competence. Is that a future seat in this room, or a different room entirely?
vii.
Whose face holds the lectern?
One persona per course, the faculty member's own licensed likeness, or a face the student chooses from an approved set. Consent, identity, and institutional policy travel with the answer.
viii.
How far may an aside wander?
The presenter answers inside the master and the deck. The latitude beyond that edge, and the moment it must say ask your professor, is a dial for you to set, not for us to assume.
ix.
Does the house review come before faculty, or beside them?
We default to your instructional designers vetting the master first, then faculty. Some institutions may want faculty's eyes first, or both at once. The order of operations is yours to set.
x.
How hard do we push — invited, or expected?
Faculty fear of replacement is the real adoption risk, and the cure is starting with volunteers. But for some course types there is a point where invitation should become the default. How fast to cross from opt-in to expected is a cultural call only you can make.

Better lectures are nice.
Better teaching, made by teachers, is the institution.
